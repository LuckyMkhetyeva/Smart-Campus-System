Smart Campus Attendance and Appointment System

Overview

The Smart Campus Attendance and Appointment System is designed to improve how educational institutions manage attendance tracking and academic appointment scheduling. The system involves several stakeholders who either interact directly with the system or benefit from the data it provides.

Understanding stakeholder roles, needs, and concerns is critical to designing a system that meets the expectations of all users. Each stakeholder group has different priorities, and the system must balance these needs while maintaining usability, reliability, and efficiency.

Stakeholder Identification and Analysis
Students

Role:
Students are primary users of the system who book appointments with lecturers and access their attendance records.

Key Concerns:
Students require a simple and accessible platform that allows them to easily book consultation appointments and review their attendance history.

Pain Points:
Students often experience difficulty contacting lecturers or finding available consultation times. Manual attendance records also make it difficult for students to track their participation in classes.

Success Metrics:
Students should be able to book appointments within one minute and access attendance information instantly.

Priority Level: High
Influence: Medium
Interest: High

Lecturers

Role:
Lecturers record student attendance and manage student consultation appointments through the system.

Key Concerns:
Lecturers require a reliable system that allows quick attendance recording and efficient appointment management.

Pain Points:
Manual attendance systems are time-consuming and prone to errors. Scheduling consultations through email or informal communication can lead to confusion and double bookings.

Success Metrics:
Lecturers should be able to record attendance in less than ten seconds and manage appointment approvals quickly.

Priority Level: High
Influence: High
Interest: High

System Administrator

Role:
The system administrator manages system configuration, user accounts, and overall system performance.

Key Concerns:
Administrators need a system that provides secure access control, reliable operation, and efficient management tools.

Pain Points:
Without centralized systems, administrators may struggle to manage large volumes of user data and system access permissions.

Success Metrics:
The system should maintain uptime above 99% and allow administrators to manage users effectively.

Priority Level: High
Influence: High
Interest: Medium

University Management

Role:
University management oversees institutional operations and relies on system data to monitor academic engagement and performance.

Key Concerns:
Management requires accurate attendance data and reliable reporting tools for decision-making.

Pain Points:
When attendance information is scattered across departments or recorded manually, generating institutional reports becomes difficult.

Success Metrics:
Reports related to attendance and appointments should be generated within five seconds.

Priority Level: Medium
Influence: High
Interest: Medium

IT Support Staff

Role:
IT staff maintain the system infrastructure and ensure that the platform operates smoothly.

Key Concerns:
They require a system that is easy to maintain, update, and troubleshoot.

Pain Points:
Poorly designed systems with limited documentation can increase maintenance difficulty and system downtime.

Success Metrics:
System updates should be deployed within one hour and technical issues should be resolved quickly.

Priority Level: Medium
Influence: Medium
Interest: Medium

Academic Advisors

Role:
Academic advisors monitor student academic progress and engagement using attendance data.

Key Concerns:
Advisors need access to accurate and up-to-date attendance information to identify students who may require support.

Pain Points:
Without centralized systems, advisors lack visibility into student attendance patterns and engagement levels.

Success Metrics:
Advisors should be able to access attendance statistics instantly.

Priority Level: Medium
Influence: Low
Interest: High

Stakeholder Influence and Interest Analysis

Stakeholders differ in terms of their influence over the system and their level of interest in its functionality.

High Influence and High Interest: Lecturers and system administrators. These stakeholders directly interact with the system and significantly influence its success.

High Interest but Lower Influence: Students and academic advisors. These users rely heavily on the system but have limited control over its implementation.

High Influence but Moderate Interest: University management. They influence institutional decisions but may not use the system daily.

Moderate Influence and Interest: IT support staff, who ensure technical reliability and maintenance.
